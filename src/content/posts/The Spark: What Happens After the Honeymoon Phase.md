Remember the beginning? When your heart raced every time their name popped up on your phone. When you stayed up until 3 a.m. talking about nothing and everything. When the mere sight of them could turn a bad day into a good one.

It’s intoxicating, that early stage. The chemicals, the uncertainty, the thrill. But here’s the thing no one tells you: **that feeling isn’t supposed to last forever.**

And that’s actually good news.

---

## The Myth of the Eternal Flame

We’ve been sold a fairy tale. The idea that if you’ve found “the one,” the passion will burn just as bright on year ten as it did on day ten. When it inevitably dims, we panic. We think something’s wrong. We start looking for the exit.

But real love isn’t a constant blaze. It’s more like a campfire. Sometimes it roars. Sometimes it crackles quietly. And sometimes you have to add wood and gently blow on the embers to keep it alive.

I’ve watched friends throw away perfectly good relationships because they mistook calm for dead. They confused comfort with boredom. They forgot that the butterflies you feel in year one aren’t the same thing as the peace you feel in year five—and peace isn’t a downgrade.

---

## Where the Spark Really Lives

A few years ago, I talked to couples who’d been together for over two decades. I asked them the same question: *How do you keep the spark alive?*

Their answers surprised me. Not one mentioned grand gestures or tropical getaways. Instead, they talked about the small stuff.

“He still makes me coffee every morning, even though I’ve never asked him to.”  
“We have this thing—every Sunday night, we sit on the porch and just… talk. No phones. No TV. Just us.”  
“She leaves little notes in my lunch bag. After thirty years, she still does that.”

The spark, it turns out, doesn’t live in candlelit dinners or expensive gifts. It lives in the in-between moments. The inside jokes. The way you still hold hands after a decade. The choice to turn toward each other instead of away.

---

## The Art of Staying Curious

One of the biggest killers of long-term love is the assumption that you already know everything about your partner.

You don’t. People change. They grow. The person you married at twenty-five is not the same person at forty-five—and if you’re not curious about who they’re becoming, you’ll wake up one day next to a stranger.

I have a friend who, every few months, asks her husband: *What’s one thing you’ve been thinking about lately that I don’t know about?* It’s a simple question, but it keeps the door open. It says: I’m still interested. I still want to discover you.

That’s the spark. Not fireworks. Just… ongoing interest.

---

## A Story of Rekindling

A couple I know—let’s call them Anna and Tom—hit a rough patch around year seven. Two kids, two demanding jobs, zero energy for each other. They’d lie in bed scrolling phones, backs turned, the silence heavy.

One night, Anna broke down. Not angry—just sad. She said, “I miss you.”

Tom didn’t have a grand solution. But the next day, he texted her during work: *Dinner tonight? Just us. I’ll figure out a sitter.*

It wasn’t a fancy restaurant. They went to the same pizza place they’d gone to on their third date. They talked about stupid stuff—his coworker’s bad jokes, her mom’s latest obsession. By the end of the night, something had shifted. Not a huge explosion of passion, just… a crack in the wall.

They started doing it once a month. No agenda, no expectations. Just two people remembering why they chose each other.

They’re not perfect now. They still argue about whose turn it is to do the dishes. But they’re okay. More than okay.

---

## The Truth About Sparks

Here’s what I’ve learned: the spark isn’t a thing you find. It’s a thing you make.

It’s choosing to kiss your partner goodbye even when you’re annoyed. It’s noticing when they’re tired and making them tea without being asked. It’s saying “thank you” for the little things, even after all these years.

It’s not always exciting. Sometimes it’s boring. Sometimes it’s hard.

But if you keep tending the fire, it will keep you warm.
